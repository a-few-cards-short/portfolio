character design

Though this medieval guard feels like a cell-shaded render of a 3D character, it's flat 2D vector art. The style also employs a coloured outline technique seen in pixel art best practices. In a game setting, other town guards could use this paper doll template with different weapons, surcoats, helmets, etc. Reuse as much as possible, is my mantra.

I actually suck at realistic character poses so I traced over a Phil Bourassa Aquaman drawing and made it my own. Reference art to the rescue!  :-)

Small on screen characters require simplicity to present well so this alien, surface dwelling visitor, and his mermaid companion, have very minimal detail. And then it dawned on me that eyes can express more than people realize so I made an experimental sprite sheet showing combinations of cheek, brow and eyelid positions. The results were many very nuanced emotional states. Couple this with emojis and game context, and the emotions will translate very well on screen.

It's worth noting how perfect these character designs are for simple 2D bone animation!

The first indie game I ever bought was Aquaria (2007) by Alec Holowka (Night in the Woods) (a Winnipeg boy! R.I.P.) and Derek Yu (Spelunky) which inspired the underwater setting for the sprite sheet above.

geometric minions

I created these tile-based dungeon crawler graphics for my son, long ago. In order for the characters to fill up a square tile fully, I used knock-kneed, hunched poses. This purposeful geometric style allowed for much faster art production.

These hideous characters were the product of my young son's (possibly concerning) demented mind. I'm so proud of him!  <:-)

pixelated simplicity

Pixel art is usually the go to for 2D graphics because of nostalgia and a lower barrier of entry. Couple pixel art with simplistic character design and the barrier is even lower. Pixel art doesn't need to be elaborate; just effective.

The effort behind pixel art is dependent on resolution. I recommend lowering the resolution as much as possible. With higher fidelity, the higher a player's expectations become. Balancing what is feasible with what is desirable over the course of development is the real challenge to completing a quality project.

In this example, the lower resolution does not omit any important details. If time was a factor, the lower resolution design would be the correct choice.

With most of my art, I try to impose restrictions to make the process more economical. Scope creep can happen with art just as easily as with programming. However, restrictions also create a canvas for creativity as we push self-imposed boundaries.

Limitations breed creativity!
