3d modelling

I'm familiar with a variety of 3D modelling applications, but I worked extensively with CAD software. However, because I would take my designs through to the final stages of manufacturing, I was able to design without compromising aesthetics, strength and comfort. I'm not a master goldsmith, but I am capable and those cross-discipline skills allowed me to become an expert jewellery modeller. Versatility is a strength.

Did you know that NURBS models have infinite detail?
No matter how close you zoom in, the curved edges and surfaces remain smooth (like how 2D vector graphics work). That's a fact, Jack!

The locket ring had a hidden clasp that snapped firmly shut. When closed, you wouldn't even know it was a locket!

When designing jewellery, it's hard to keep the level of detail down. You are working on something that looks like a bracelet on your monitor, but is a small ring in reality. The simplicity in my designs are purposeful and necessary.

I used a high resolution 3D resin printer, a 4-axis CNC milling machine, a 3D scanner and a fiber laser engraver to cut into metal with ultra-fine details; all of which requires a technical mind in order operate and push the limits of what is possible. I wore many hats and everything I learned made me that much better at my core job.

And I never stopped learning!  :-)

low-poly goodness

I created some rudimentary characters for a demonic corporate comedy project called Evil Encorporated. The modelling is intentionally boxy and very simplistic, but I was still pleased with the proportions and speed at which I created the models. This was my first time using the excellent low-poly modeller Blockbench.

Those minions look eager to work for the "big guy" downstairs!

voxelicious

I tasked myself with a challenge to create the simplest voxel character for a dungeon crawler. There's a certain charm to limited detail and allowing the player's mind to fill in the rest. As long as artistic intention is evident, simplicity can still be attractive.

Nothing tops MagicaVoxel for efficient and robust voxel modelling and painting.

If the Atari 2600 could render 3D, this is probably what it would look like.  ;-)
